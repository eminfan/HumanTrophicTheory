# Chapter 5 Group Formation: Meme Domestication and Identity Construction

## 5.1 Meme Domestication Mechanism

### Core Proposition

**The system transforms discrete individual energy into group entropy reduction (order) through meme domestication.**

Meme domestication is not forced by violence, but through rule design, semantic implantation, and cognitive packaging, making individuals actively direct their behavior towards group interests. This is the core mechanism for maintaining order in social systems:

### Essence of Memes

- **Definition**: Memes are the basic units of culture, which are thoughts, behaviors, symbols, etc., transmitted through imitation.
- **Function**: Memes are the "operating system" of the social system, responsible for the construction of rules, institutions, and narratives.
- **Characteristics**: Memes have the characteristics of self-replication, variation, and selection, similar to genes.

### Goals of Meme Domestication

1. **Energy Transformation**: Transforming individual dispersed energy into group orderly energy.
2. **Behavior Coordination**: Making individual behavior consistent with group interests.
3. **Order Maintenance**: Achieving social stability through cognitive unification.

### Three Core Mechanisms of Meme Domestication

#### 1. Selective Redundancy (Redundancy Shielding Law)

**Definition**: The system deliberately sets non-essential complex rules that exceed the cost required to solve the problem. Its true function is not to solve problems, but to screen for compliant individuals.

**Operational Logic**:
- The more complex the rules, the more individual energy is consumed.
- Complex rules require significant learning costs.
- The higher the learning cost, the less likely individuals are to question the system.
- Independent thinkers are eliminated by complex rules.
- Compliant individuals pass the rule screening and enter the system.

**Typical Cases**:
- Ancient Imperial Examinations: Extremely formatted requirements like the Eight-legged Essay screened for civil servants who absolutely obeyed central decrees.
- Modern Workplace: Lengthy reimbursement processes or meetings screen for employees who can tolerate 996 and obey management.
- Legal System: Complex legal provisions make it difficult for ordinary people to understand and question.

**Countermeasures**:
- Calculate the "screening cost" of redundancy.
- Choose "low-cost compatibility"—follow core rules, ignore redundant processes.
- Protect individual energy, rather than being passively trained.

#### 2. Semantic Anchoring (Semantic Anchoring Law)

**Definition**: The system locks individual cognition within a specific framework through specific vocabulary, symbols, or narratives, making it impossible to see the true structure of the system.

**Operational Logic**:
- Semantic anchors reduce individual thinking costs.
- Anchors become the "default framework" for cognition.
- Any thinking that deviates from the anchor is considered "abnormal".
- Anchors unify group cognition and reduce system entropy.

**Typical Cases**:
- Political Terminology: Words like "freedom", "democracy", and "justice" are given specific meanings.
- Economic Terminology: Standardized definitions of concepts like "success", "efficiency", and "competition".
- Cultural Symbols: Flags, anthems, festivals, etc., enhance group identity.

**Countermeasures**:
- Semantic De-anchoring: Identify the true function of vocabulary.
- Multidimensional Thinking: Examine the same concept from different perspectives.
- Redefinition: Express true feelings in your own language.

#### 3. False Subjectivity (Entropy-Energy Transformation Law)

**Definition**: Packaging group energy-consuming behaviors as "self-realization" or "autonomous choice" to reduce resistance to energy transformation.

**Operational Logic**:
- The system packages behaviors needed by the group as individual "free choices".
- Through ideological propaganda, individuals believe that behavior is out of their own will.
- Individuals actively contribute energy to the system under the illusion of "autonomous choice".
- The system obtains stable energy input, while individuals obtain psychological satisfaction.

**Typical Cases**:
- Consumerism: Packaging purchasing behavior as "personality expression" and "self-realization".
- Workplace Culture: Packaging overtime as "struggle spirit" and "career development".
- Political Participation: Packaging voting as "democratic rights" and "civic responsibility".

**Countermeasures**:
- Energy Tracking: Monitor your own energy flow.
- Motivation Analysis: Distinguish between real needs and needs instilled by the system.
- Autonomous Decision: Make choices based on real needs.

## 5.2 Identity Construction Mechanism

### Core Proposition

**Group identity is the product of meme domestication and the psychological basis for individuals to integrate into groups.**

### Levels of Identity

1. **Personal Identity**: Individual's cognition of their own identity.
2. **Group Identity**: Individual's sense of belonging to the group.
3. **Cultural Identity**: Individual's identification with a specific culture.

### Steps of Identity Construction

1. **Classification**: Dividing individuals into different groups.
2. **Labeling**: Giving specific symbols and characteristics to the group.
3. **Comparison**: Emphasizing differences inside and outside the group.
4. **Reinforcement**: Reinforcing group identity through rituals and narratives.

### Functions of Identity

1. **Cohesion Function**: Enhancing internal cohesion of the group.
2. **Normative Function**: Guiding individual behavior to conform to group norms.
3. **Protection Function**: Providing a psychological safety net for individuals.
4. **Mobilization Function**: Inspiring individuals to act for group interests.

## 5.3 Evolutionary Logic of Groups

### Core Proposition

**The formation and evolution of groups are strategic choices for gene groups to maximize their own fitness.**

### Types of Groups

1. **Kinship Groups**: Groups based on blood relations (such as families, clans).
2. **Regional Groups**: Groups based on geographical space (such as communities, countries).
3. **Cultural Groups**: Groups based on cultural identity (such as ethnic groups, religions).
4. **Interest Groups**: Groups based on common interests (such as companies, political parties).

### Evolutionary Mechanisms of Groups

1. **Group Formation**: Individuals form groups for survival and development.
2. **Group Competition**: Resource competition between different groups.
3. **Group Selection**: Characteristics beneficial to group survival are retained.
4. **Group Evolution**: Group structure and function evolve with environmental changes.

### Boundaries of Groups

- **Physical Boundaries**: Physical boundaries such as geography and organization.
- **Cultural Boundaries**: Cultural boundaries such as language and values.
- **Psychological Boundaries**: Psychological boundaries such as identity and belonging.
